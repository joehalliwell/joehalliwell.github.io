Eulogy

In his last few weeks in hospital, my Dad took to greeting me with a fist bump. I have no idea where that came from — it might have been an accident the first time — but we both recognized it as something new. He smiled. We enjoyed the absurdity of it. We were happy to see each other.

As a child, I discovered that I could produce an Adam Ant-inspired yodel that would make my Dad block his ears and screw up his face in anguish. I can only have been five or six, and I revelled in the power it gave me.

My dad believed in democracy. He worked as an Electoral Registration Officer and used to deliver polling cards on foot. I'd go with him to help.

Tranmere Park estate was the best beat. There are grand houses there in mock tudor and modernist. The letterboxes snapped at my fingers.

My dad paid me for helping him. I thought it was just a fair day's pay for a fair day's work.

My dad supported the miners. We argued about that. I couldn't fathom prolonging that grubby business. He saw beyond the coal face.

The only car he ever drove was a sage green Vauxhall cavalier.

When I was small and in the passenger seat we'd sing silly songs: "we march to our places, with clean hands faces and pay great attention to all we are told; for we know we can never be happy or clever, for learning is worth more than silver and gold".

That sounds like a hymn to obedience doesn't it? But the two of us sang it with wild abandon and a twinkle in the eye.

As a teenager I am in the back seat, it is always night and the rain is always crackling against the windows. It is always Sunday and he is driving us back home from Steph and Jim's, or Margaret and Graham's, or our wider family down in Manchester.

Like a ship in a storm, the radio crashes through thick waves of static, surfacing occasionally as football results. It is too dark to read so I listen to the radio and stare at the black moorland and sometimes distant city lights deep in the valley.

"Roger!"

My mum shouts at him occasionally to keep him from nodding off.

Once whilst rifling through his sock drawer looking for something or other I found a birthday card I'd made for him. It depicts him as a golden-maned lion, which — if you knew my timid and raven-haired dad — is comically inappropriate.

It was a child's idea of what a birthday card for a father ought to look like. And even as a child I knew that: I was trying it on for size. Or maybe I just wanted to draw a lion.

Still, I was surprised — and pleased — that he'd kept that card with his socks.

In the 90s, I rifled through his wardrobe. His least favourite clothes became my fashion statements. A kipper tie with a seemingly irregular grid of black and white squares like a crossword puzzle. A cosy blue corduroy bomber jacket. A jazzy cream shirt with brown stripes like a musical stave.

They became mine. I built my style around his.

We both loved puzzles, especially cryptic crosswords. And he was good at them.

Earlier this year, when we tried to do a puzzle together, he refused to believe I could get stuck on anything. He thought I was putting it on to make him feel better. "Aww don't play, Joe, don't play" he said in his anguished voice.

This time it hurt me too.

These few episodes of anguish aside he seemed quite indifferent to my actions and choices.

I wouldn't have had it any other way.

I have always felt free to draw a lion if I want to.

Fist bump, Dad.
